* Notes

This book presents ten practical principles for developing a Stoic mindset, drawn from the teachings
of ancient philosophers like Epictetus, Marcus Aurelius, and Seneca, and applied to modern life
challenges.

** Chapter 1: Principle 1 - Adversity as Fuel
/Epigraph: "The impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way becomes the way." ~
Marcus Aurelius/

This chapter explores how Stoicism was born from adversity and how setbacks can become sources of
strength. Tuitert shares his own experience of missing the 2002 Olympics due to overtraining and how
this setback ultimately led him to discover Stoic philosophy.

** Chapter 2: Principle 2 - Understanding Our Emotions and Judgments
/Epigraph: "It is not things in themselves that trouble us, but our judgments of them." ~ Epictetus/

This chapter examines the Stoic approach to emotions, emphasizing that emotions arise from our
judgments about events rather than the events themselves.

*** Room for Emotions (p. 30)
The chapter clarifies a common misconception about Stoicism - that Stoics suppress emotions.
Instead, they seek to understand them.

#+begin_quote
According to the Stoics, emotions are the result of our judgments about events. An emotion is
fundamentally neither good nor bad. It's a reflection of your judgment and your thoughts about a
situation.
#+end_quote

*** Acting vs. Reacting (p. 30-31)
#+begin_quote
The Stoics try not to act on immediate emotion. In other words: they endeavor to not make choices
based on frustration, rage, and euphoria. Instead, they try to understand the thinking and judgments
behind their emotions so as to make better decisions.
#+end_quote

*** First Impressions vs. Emotions (p. 31-32)
The chapter distinguishes between immediate impressions (which we cannot control) and the emotions
that follow (which we can influence through our judgments).

#+begin_quote
The Stoic answers that even a wise person still feels first impressions, but they don't convert
those impressions into emotions. A wise person can be unnerved for a moment, and then gathers
themself quickly. By applying a Stoic mindset, you learn to feel fear, just like everybody else,
while choosing not to go along with that first impression. You don't suppress this fear, but you
take control of it by not reacting to that first impression with your thoughts. The result is that
you can stay calm and act accordingly.
#+end_quote

*** Examining Our Emotions (p. 32)
#+begin_quote
One of the basic principles of a Stoic mindset is that you consider your emotions. You can worry
about the future, but a Stoic will ask themself: Why? Why are you scared of losing your job? Why are
you jealous? What is the judgment underlying that emotion? Might you be angry because, in your view,
someone did a great injustice to you? And what is this feeling of injustice based on?
#+end_quote

** Chapter 3: Principle 3 - Control Only What You Can Control
/Epigraph: "Do not seek to have events happen as you wish, but wish them to happen as they do happen,
and all will be well with you." ~ Epictetus/

This chapter presents one of the most fundamental Stoic principles: the dichotomy of control.

*** The Fundamental Principle (p. 45)
#+begin_quote
One of the foremost principles of a Stoic mindset is that you can divide things into that which you
can control and that which you can't.
#+end_quote

The chapter uses Epictetus's story to illustrate this principle. Born into slavery, he learned that
protesting circumstances beyond his control only gave his oppressors more power.

*** The Danger of False Control (p. 45-46)
#+begin_quote
And he had a strong point: those who let their actions be determined by circumstances they couldn't
control, allowed themselves to be enslaved by those circumstances. That's why Epictetus found the
divide between what you could and couldn't control paramount.
#+end_quote

*** Accepting Reality (p. 36)
#+begin_quote
Do not seek to have events happen as you wish, but wish them to happen as they do happen, and all
will be well with you.

~ Epictetus
#+end_quote

#+begin_quote
Epictetus teaches us to make the best of every situation, and not just that: he teaches us to love
every situation. It's foolish and pointless to wish things were different than they are. For a
start, it's simple logic—"wishing for figs in winter," he calls it. We can't deny life's reality.
#+end_quote

** Chapter 4: Principle 4 - Community and Connection
This chapter explores the Stoic understanding of our interconnectedness with others and our
responsibilities to the community.

*** Chrysippus: The Athletic Philosopher (p. 57-58)
#+begin_quote
The most important successor to Zeno, the founder of Stoicism, was Chrysippus of Soli, who lived in
the third century BC. He was, besides a philosopher, a passionate runner, and also studied under
Cleanthes, a passionate boxer. See that? Sports and philosophy make an outstanding combination.
Chrysippus was rather fanatic in his work as a philosopher: throughout his life he wrote more than
700 works. He was a brilliant thinker and expert in argumentation, with which he lay a sturdy
logical foundation beneath Stoic philosophy. He was also one of the first with the ambition and
self-confidence to spread Stoic thought beyond the traditional gates of the Agora in Athens. Without
Chrysippus we wouldn't know of Stoic philosophy today.
#+end_quote

*** Ethical Competition (p. 58-59)
#+begin_quote
According to Chrysippus, a runner should do their utmost to win a race, though never by tripping
another. And it's the same in life: it's good to strive for something, as long you don't do it at
the expense of another.
#+end_quote

*** Interconnectedness (p. 59-60)
#+begin_quote
Absolute autonomy doesn't exist in this sense according to the Stoics: we are all connected with one
another and work together to achieve goals—that's ingrained in our nature. Plus: What are
experiences worth if you can't share them with others?
#+end_quote

** Later Principles: Living with Virtue and Purpose

*** Reflecting on Life Well-Lived (p. 42)
#+begin_quote
Let us go to our sleep with joy and gladness; let us say: I have lived; the course which Fortune set
for me is finished.

~ Seneca
#+end_quote

*** The Regret of Unlived Lives (p. 45)
#+begin_quote
Bronnie Ware, a caregiver who worked with dying people, asked them what, looking back on their
lives, they regretted, and went on to write the book The Top Five Regrets of the Dying. The number
one regret? "I wish I'd had the courage to live a life true to myself, not the life others expected
of me"—
#+end_quote

*** Good Stoic Resolutions (p. 46)
#+begin_quote
GOOD STOIC RESOLUTIONS

Every year we promise ourselves to do new things, adding even more items to the long list of
resolutions we didn't get around to doing last year but now are really going to do. What would
happen if we turned this around and instead asked ourselves what we wanted to do less of? We could
spend more time and energy on things that help us move forward and really matter.
#+end_quote

*** Finding Purpose Through Daily Work (p. 60)
#+begin_quote
So forget for a second the meaning of purpose, which has become a buzzword. Forget describing a big
goal. Forget following your passion. You'll discover those passions and those goals after first
establishing your direction. The trick is doing what aligns with your nature, is finding that
problem you aren't forced to face but above all want to wrestle with every day. Something you would
also do if, at least in the short term, you weren't paid to do it. That which we do every day
determines who we become. That's where fulfillment lies: in the work of every day.
#+end_quote

** On Virtue and Right Action

*** The Simplicity of Right Action (p. 64)
#+begin_quote
Just that you do the right thing. The rest doesn't matter.

~ Marcus Aurelius
#+end_quote

*** The Four Cardinal Virtues (p. 66)
#+begin_quote
Almost every one of these heroes' good traits can be traced back to the following cardinal virtues:
￼ Courage to overcome the fear holding you back from taking action. The guts to swim against the
stream. Courage to endure illness, loss, or a pandemic without giving in to complaint or to wishing
things were different. Temperance to know how much you truly need of something. Knowing when you
have enough and are able to bring your life into balance and to regulate your emotions. Justice to
act rightly in relation to other people. Standing up for the weaker and being equally as nice to the
secretary as to the CEO. Wisdom to make the distinction between what is good and bad, what is right
to strive for in everyday life and what is not.
#+end_quote

** On Learning and Practice

*** Learning for Life (p. 69)
#+begin_quote
We do not learn for school, but for life.

~ Seneca
#+end_quote

*** Philosophy in Action (p. 71)
#+begin_quote
The Stoics aren't concerned with winning arguments, working out abstract theories, or, like
scholars, searching for truth on paper. The value of philosophy lies in practice: the Stoic mindset
centers on applying theory in an active life—in politics, as a parent, as a citizen, as a human
being.
#+end_quote

** Key Themes
- **Adversity as teacher**: Setbacks and obstacles can become sources of strength and wisdom
- **Emotional intelligence**: Understanding that our judgments, not events, create our emotions
- **The dichotomy of control**: Focus energy only on what we can actually influence
- **Community responsibility**: We are interconnected and have obligations to others
- **Virtue over outcomes**: Doing right matters more than results
- **Practical philosophy**: Stoicism is meant to be lived, not just studied
