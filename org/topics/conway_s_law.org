#+title: Conway's Law
#+filetags: :software:architecture:

#+begin_quote
Organizations which design systems are constrained to produce designs which are copies of the communication structures of those organizations.
— Melvin Conway, 1968
#+end_quote

* Overview
Conway's Law is the observation that technical artifacts reflect the communication structures of the teams that build them. A layered architecture naturally emerges from organisations where UI, backend, and database teams sit in separate departments — because that's how those teams communicate.

The law works in both directions: architecture choice reinforces team structure (layered → technical silos) and team structure reinforces architecture choice. This feedback loop is why changing one without changing the other rarely works.

* Inverse Conway Maneuver
Coined by Jonny Leroy of Thoughtworks: *deliberately evolve team structures to promote the desired architecture.* If you want microservices, form small cross-functional teams aligned to services before you split the system. If you form the teams right, the architecture will follow.

This insight is now commonly known as *team topologies* — the study of how team communication patterns and system architectures co-evolve. The book /Team Topologies/ (Skelton & Pais, 2019) formalises this into four team types: stream-aligned, enabling, complicated-subsystem, and platform.

* Implications for architecture partitioning
- *Technical partitioning* (layered architecture) naturally pairs with technically-organised teams (UI team, backend team, DBA team). Conway's Law reinforces this: teams communicate across technical boundaries, so the architecture mirrors those boundaries.
- *Domain partitioning* (modular monolith, microservices) requires and reinforces cross-functional teams aligned to business domains. The Inverse Conway Maneuver says: if you want domain-partitioned architecture, form domain-aligned teams first.
