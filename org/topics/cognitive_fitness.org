#+title: Cognitive Fitness
#+filetags: :psychology:health:technology:

The idea that mental faculties — concentration, critical thinking, contemplation — require
deliberate exercise and protection, just as physical fitness does. Coined as a cultural
frame by Cal Newport in a 2026 NYT essay, drawing an explicit parallel to the physical
fitness revolution sparked by Eisenhower's 1955 heart attack and Dr. Kenneth Cooper's
/Aerobics/ (1968).

* Core argument

We are in a cognitive health crisis analogous to the physical health crisis of the mid-20th
century. Digital technology — especially short-form content and AI tools — is degrading
our capacity to think deeply. A cultural revolution, similar to the one that transformed how
we think about diet and exercise, is both necessary and possible.

* Evidence of decline

- Gloria Mark (UC Irvine): attention spans are ~1/3 of what they were in 2004; biggest
  drops around 2012, coinciding with smartphone ubiquity
- Rising share of US adults struggling with basic reading/math over the past decade
- 18-year-olds reporting difficulty thinking and concentrating increased in the same period
- Meta-analysis (2025): short-form video (TikTok, Instagram) associated with poorer
  cognition and reduced attention
- 2023 experiment: mere presence of a smartphone in the room significantly reduced
  concentration ability
- AI study (2026, 600+ participants): significant negative correlation between frequent AI
  tool usage and critical thinking abilities
- Brain activity study: "brain connectivity systematically scaled down with the amount of
  external support" (LLM-assisted writing)

* The ultraprocessed content analogy

Just as ultraprocessed food (Doritos, Pop-Tarts) is engineered to be irresistible at the
cost of nutrition, ultraprocessed content (TikTok, Instagram, X) is algorithmically
recombined user-generated content designed to be irresistible at the cost of cognition.

/What is a TikTok video if not a digital Dorito?/

Recommendation: avoid ultraprocessed content as strongly as ultraprocessed food — not
most of the time, but most people, most of the time.

* The contemplation = cardio analogy

| Physical fitness        | Cognitive fitness                         |
|-------------------------+-------------------------------------------|
| Ultraprocessed food     | Short-form algorithmic content            |
| Aerobic exercise        | Contemplation (sustained focused thought) |
| Sedentary lifestyle     | Constant companion phone use              |
| Diet guidelines         | "Digital nutrition" norms                 |
| School PE requirements  | School phone bans                         |

* Practical prescriptions

** Avoid ultraprocessed content
No TikTok, Instagram, X as regular habits. The sugar-high benefits don't outweigh the
cognitive cost.

** Reading as cognitive cardio
Maryanne Wolf: "deep reading processes" rewire neuronal regions toward greater
complexity and nuance. /Deep reading is our species' bridge to insight and novel thought./
Proposed heuristic: a few dozen book pages/day = the new 10,000 daily steps.

** Reject the constant companion model
Keeping a phone within reach causes motivational neurons (trained to expect a reward
from checking) to fire constantly, making sustained contemplation a battle of willpower.
Simple rule: charge your phone in the kitchen, not your pocket.

** Institutional interventions
- School phone bans: NBER 2025 working paper found "significant improvements" in test
  scores; Dutch study (317 high schools): 75% reported improved focus, 67% improved
  social climate
- Meeting phone bans (Skift pre-pandemic experiment): increased communication,
  reduced hiding behind screens
- Workplace: reduce hyperactive email/Slack loops (→ [[id:3c7f407e-38f4-4a55-bfca-89e2e09f65a9][Deep Work]])

** AI use: preserve cognitive load
- Good AI use: automating routine/repetitive tasks → reduces burnout
- Bad AI use: offloading core thinking tasks → "brain fry" (Boston Consulting Group
  study) + "workslop" (HBR term for AI-generated work masquerading as good work)
- Rule: /your writing should be your own/. The strain of crafting a memo is a mental
  gym workout, not an annoyance to eliminate.
